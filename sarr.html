Search and Rescue Robot

As part of MAE 322: Mechanical Design with Professor Daniel Nosenchuck, I worked in a group of 11 people on an 8-week project to design, manufacture, and automate a SaRR capable of surviving a 1-foot drop test, tracking light, navigating a ramp and chute, breaching a 1-foot wall, and delivering a medical payload to a randomized drop point. Our robot took inspiration from biological systems, allowing us to develop novel solutions to the engineering challenges posed by the course.

As Electrical Lead, I designed and assembled the full wiring harnesses for both robots and took point on systems issues during programming, testing, and debugging.

Marsupial Robots: MotherBot and BabyBot

Inspired by pouched marsupials, MotherBot and BabyBot work as a team, allowing each subsystem to optimize for different tasks. MotherBot uses her bulk to handles the drop test and ramp, then navigates the chute, reaches over the wall, and places BabyBot safely on the other side, ready to identify the drop point and deliver the medkit.

This dual design avoids compromises on durability or stability that more typical wall-breach methods require. MotherBot protects BabyBot during the drop test using a tensioned line so the large moment is applied through the reinforced drivetrain rather than the more fragile medkit launcher mechanism.

The Process

1) Brainstorming

Early days were spent brainstorming, focusing on feasibility, reliability, and uniqueness. A decision matrix helped pare down ideas into a few key choices.

2) Design

Initial designs were CADded in Creo and iterated aggressively through prototyping and failure. Failure can be a frustrating process, but we worked to aggressively iterate and use our failures as momentum towards success. Fail fast and fail forward, as our project manager Ella would always say! We also took this time to plan out a semester-long task schedule to ensure we hit our deadlines on time.

3) Manufacturing

Robots were constructed from laser-cut acrylic, 3D-printed PET, welded steel, and machined aluminum using Princeton’s MAE machine shop. Manufacturing took place on a rolling basis as CAD designs were finalized. As an Electrical Engineer, it is very unusual to get access to the machine shop, and I really enjoyed the hands on process.

4) Electrical & Software

Both robots used a Teensy 4.1. Schematics, wiring harnesses, and software were built in parallel with manufacturing to enable rapid integration. Each robot had its own unique sensor arrays and power requirements. Notable is MotherBot's hammerhead shark inspired sensor mount for multidirectional distance sensing and light tracking.

6) Demo Day!

With time up and robots ready, it was time to demo.

We were unfortunately unable to complete the course in its entirety due to unprecedented overheating problems with the motor controllers on the day of the demo (this was a critical mistake I made, assuming the MCs would be fine because they worked during test runs without rigorously stress testing them at high current load), but the robots succesfully managed to complete every part of the course individually. The whole team put in a monumental amount of effort into the completion of this project, fighting flu season and finals to get it done, and I couldn't be prouder of them.
